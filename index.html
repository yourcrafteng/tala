Understanding Congenital AML

A rare leukemia present at or near birth. This page exists to raise awareness, share what's known, and point families and supporters toward trusted resources.

What it is

What is congenital AML?

Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is a cancer of the blood and bone marrow, where the body produces large numbers of abnormal blood cells. When leukemia is diagnosed in the first weeks of life, it is called congenital leukemia — a very rare condition, generally defined in medical literature as leukemia diagnosed between birth and about four weeks of age. In newborns, AML is the most common form.

Congenital AML is rare, estimated to occur in roughly 1 to 5 newborns per million births. Because it begins so early, its signs can appear at or shortly after birth — and they can look like those of other, more common newborn conditions, which is one reason awareness matters. The disease is strongly linked to specific genetic changes, which doctors use to understand its risk and guide treatment.

This page is for awareness and general information only. It is not medical advice and cannot replace a doctor. If you have any concern about a child's health, please speak with a qualified medical professional. For detailed information on diagnosis, treatment, and outlook, see the trusted organizations linked below.

What to know

Signs and symptoms

The signs of congenital AML can be easy to miss or mistaken for other, more common newborn conditions. Many of the symptoms below have causes that have nothing to do with leukemia. They are listed here for awareness, not for diagnosis.

  • Pale skin, or looking unusually tired or weak
  • Easy bruising or bleeding
  • Small flat red or purple spots under the skin (petechiae)
  • Firmness or swelling in the abdomen
  • Unusual skin lumps or patches — sometimes blue, purple, or green (leukemia cutis)
  • Fever
  • Poor feeding or loss of appetite
Only a doctor can determine what is causing any of these signs. If you notice anything about a child's health that worries you, contact a qualified medical professional. Most of the time these symptoms are caused by something other than leukemia — but they should always be checked by someone qualified to assess them.

Our story

Tala's story

November 11, 2025 — February 3, 2026

Our daughter Tala was born on November 11, 2025, by emergency C-section. At first it was a mystery — she was covered in small blue spots and struggling to breathe. My wife's pregnancy had been perfect. She'd been under the care of a perinatologist. There had been no warning.

The next day, we learned that Tala had leukemia — congenital AML, with high-risk genetics.

We spent the following months with her in the hospital, helping her through chemotherapy, staying close, doing everything we could. She died on February 3, 2026, before she could begin her second round.

This page exists because of Tala. Congenital leukemia is rare, and it can arrive without any warning — even when everything about a pregnancy seems healthy. We're sharing her story, and what we learned, in the hope that we can help any other family going through this, in any way we can. If that's you: you are not alone.
